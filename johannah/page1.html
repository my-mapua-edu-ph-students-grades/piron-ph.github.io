Development of the Covered Typhoon

1. Typhoon Ulysses, also known as vamco, brought catastrophic winds and intense rains , causing flooding and landslides in CALABARZON. It originated from a low pressure area ( LPA ) in the east of Mindanao to a tropical depression on november 8, 2020 and then three days later formed into a typhoon. It made its first landfall in Patnanungan , Quezon in the philippines.

2. Through PAR or Philippine Area of Responsibility our geographical coordinates are : 5°N 115°E, 15°N 115°E, 21°N 120°E, 25°N 135°E and 5°N 135°E.

3. Typhoon Ulysses was detected at the wind speed of (245 km;150 mi) going into the philippines. As it hit the Quezon province at 10:30 pm it’s maximum sustained wind of 150 km/h and gustiness of up to 205 km/h.

  • How vulnerable are they to typhoons?

    The storm first made landfall in Patnanungan, Quezon, around 10:30 p.m. and then continued west to hit the island of Luzon, where Manila saw its worst flooding in years. A river in Marikina, located in the Manila metropolitan area, was reported to have risen a meter (3 feet) in less than three hours. As of November 12, several dams were in danger of overflowing due to the heavy rains. Tens of thousands of homes in low-lying suburbs of the national capital were submerged in roof-level floods, trapping residents in their rooftops while awaiting rescue. The rains also caused high turbidity of the water in Ipo Dam in Bulacan, leading to service interruptions as the concessionaire reduced the production in its water treatment plants. Nearly two million households were also without electricity as high winds toppled power lines. In conclusion… they were definitely VERY vulnerable.

  • Was there any damage? How severe were they?

    Vamco impacted the Philippines and Vietnam just a few days after the strike of Typhoon Goni. Both countries were still attempting to recover from Goni's initial impact, but Vamco went on to quickly exceed Goni's record as the sixth-costliest Philippine typhoon on record; in total both typhoons caused ₱37.2 billion (US$770 million) in damage in the Philippines alone.
